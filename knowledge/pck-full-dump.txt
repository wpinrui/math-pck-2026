## LECTURE 1: Teaching Approaches & Learning Theories (Yeo)

### Core Philosophy
- You teach **students**, not maths — care about whether they learn, not just content delivery
- Teaching ≠ talking; ensuring another person learns is **harder than doing it yourself**

### Skemp's Two Types of Understanding (1976)
| Type | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Relational** | Knowing *what* to do AND *why* |
| **Instrumental** | Rules without reasons |

- Instrumental understanding = students can't adapt when they forget rules
- Relational understanding → easier to reconstruct forgotten knowledge

### Why Rules Without Understanding Fail
- Students at lower readiness cannot memorise unrelated rules indefinitely
- Understanding helps retention and recall after forgetting

### Constructivism (Piaget)
- Brain links new knowledge to **existing knowledge (schema)**
- Weak links = easily forgotten
- **Assimilation**: fitting new info into existing schema (no change to schema)
- **Accommodation**: changing existing schema to fit new info
- Key idea: students **construct** their own knowledge — they are not empty vessels

### Skemp's Principle of Learning Maths (1987)
> Higher-order concepts cannot be taught by definition — only by **suitable examples**

- Start with examples, guide to definition — not the reverse
- Definition-first confuses unmotivated students and confirms their fears

### Guided-Discovery Learning (Bruner)
- Guide students to **discover** and **generalise** concepts so they construct knowledge meaningfully
- Can be teacher-centred (whole class) or student-centred (worksheets)
- Overlaps concept development with process development

### Teaching Processes
- Teach **heuristics** explicitly when going through worked examples or problems
- E.g. for trig identities: start from more complex side; think what identities apply; work backwards if stuck
- Basic knowledge must be memorised to enable thinking (Schoenfeld)

### Metacognition
- Thinking about thinking: **awareness, monitoring, self-regulation**
- Get students to **reflect** at end of every lesson
- Even one durable takeaway per lesson is sufficient

### Pentagon Model (2020 — Yeo's version)
Centre: **Mathematical Problem Solving**
Five components: Concepts · Skills · Processes · Metacognition · Dispositions/Attitudes

## LECTURE 4: Teaching Approaches & Learning Theories (Part 2)

### Bruner's C-P-A Approach
- **Concrete → Pictorial → Abstract** (hyphens = links between stages, not 3 separate methods)
- Start with concrete manipulatives (e.g. algebra tiles), move to pictorial, then abstract identity
- Visual > proof for convincing students (Key Lesson 1)
- Suitable for **lower-readiness** students — not starting with abstract proofs

### Extended C-P-A Approach
- When no suitable concrete manipulative exists, use **concrete examples (numbers)**
- Pri: real objects → pictures → numerals | Sec: numbers → (pictures) → algebra | Uni: algebra IS concrete
- What is abstract at one level becomes concrete at a higher level
- Key Lesson 2: **Always go from concrete/examples → abstract**

### Teaching Skills (Behaviourism, modified)
- Rooted in **behaviourism** (Thorndike, Skinner, Gagné) — stimulus-response bonds
- Modified by constructivism: meaningfulness matters, not just S-R conditioning
- Structure: **"We do one. You do more."**
  - WE1 done together (teacher questions students) → Practice Q1 similar to WE1 (leave WE1 on board)
  - Q2 onwards: slight increase in structure/difficulty
  - Introduce WE2 when most students can't handle next practice question
- **Copying WEs**: wastes time; Practice Q1 serves as model solution instead
- **Homework**: similar difficulty to WEs/practice (not harder); can add 1–2 challenge Qs at end
- Key Lesson 3: **There must be structure/progression in WEs, practice Qs, and HW**

### Attitudes & Dispositions
- **Beliefs → Confidence → Perseverance**: students who believe maths = <5 min will give up
  - Counter by sharing real examples (your own uni experience) + encouraging perseverance
- **Interest/Appreciation**: use *variety* of resources; aim for at least one interesting part per lesson
  - LOVE framework: Linking Opportunities in a Variety of Experiences to learning of Maths
  - Must always link engaging activity back to mathematics
- Key Lesson 4: **Engage students' hearts by making some parts of lessons interesting**

## ICT LECTURE: Use of ICT in Maths Education

### MOE ICT Masterplans (background)
- mp1 (1997–2002): basic IT infrastructure + teacher IT competency
- mp2 (2003–2008): dynamic curriculum using technology; baseline IT standards for students
- mp3 (2009–2014): ICT at the **heart** of education — integrated in lesson planning, not an add-on

### How to Use ICT
| Use | Notes |
|---|---|
| **NOT** PPT for step-by-step working | Students can't follow; can't ask questions along the way |
| Whiteboard for working | Step-by-step + questioning |
| PPT for colourful pictures | e.g. types of pyramids |
| **Explore concepts** (GSP/GeoGebra/LiveMath) | Guided-discovery (Bruner) using pre-designed templates |
| Visual aids | e.g. GSP demo of unit circle → sine curve |
| Teaching aids / emulators | e.g. show calculator key presses |
| Virtual manipulatives | e.g. balance for solving equations (handles negatives) |
| E-learning (interactive) | JavaSketchpad, GeoGebra web — content must be interactive, not just text |
| Teacher tools | Equation Editor, FXDraw3 for preparing worksheets/papers |
| Internet resources | Songs, videos to engage students |

- **8 Ways**: puzzles/games/songs/videos · pictures · explore concepts · visual aids · teaching aids · virtual manipulatives · e-learning · prepare materials
- Give students **pre-designed templates** — designing them wastes time on technical aspects, not maths
- **Hands-on > demo** where possible; demo is appropriate when content is too difficult to explore

### Why Use ICT?
- Bruner CPA: "The computer has the ability to make the abstract concrete" (Turkle & Papert, 1990)
- ICT **bridges the gap** between abstract concepts and concrete experiences
- Key Lesson 2: **Use ICT only when it enhances student learning** (e.g. making abstract more concrete); if it doesn't help, don't use it

### When to Use ICT?
- Only when it **helps student learning**
- Do NOT use if concrete manipulatives work better
- Balance: use different modes of teaching — impossible to let students discover everything

### Planning & Conducting an ICT Lesson
- Visit computer lab **a few days before**: test teacher + student computers
- Visit again **day/lesson before**: confirm equipment still works
- Try out template + worksheet beforehand — fix problems before lesson
- **Conduct**: gather students at front → demo difficult parts → hands-on → demo → hands-on (2-period = 2 segments)
- **Conclude**: gather again to consolidate and verify correct discoveries
- Key Lesson 3: **Must visit the computer lab at least a few days before your first ICT lesson**

### Overarching Principle
> Your duty is not to teach *per se*, but to teach so that **student learning** takes place. Use ICT purposefully to enhance that learning.

# Big Ideas & Proportionality — Study Sheet

## Orientations to Math Learning
- **Tool**: use math to solve problems; content chosen for real-world value
- **Discipline**: understand the nature of math; content chosen to illustrate mathematicians' thinking
- Every syllabus has both; N(T) leans tool, A-Math leans discipline

## Nature of Mathematics (PORA)
| Theme | Category |
|---|---|
| Properties & Relationships | Concepts |
| Operations & Algorithms | Skills |
| Representations & Communications | Processes |
| Abstractions & Applications | Processes |

## Big Ideas
- Central ideas that **link** topics, strands, and levels into a coherent whole (Charles, 2005)
- 8 clusters (Sec): **I**nvariance, **F**unctions, **D**iagrams, **N**otations, **P**roportionality, **M**easures, **M**odels, **E**quivalence
- Primary: 6 clusters (exclude Functions & Models) — **IMPEND**
- "Proportionality" = a **cluster** of big ideas; teach by forming **statements about** a big idea

---

## Ratio
- Compares **relative sizes** of ≥2 quantities of the **same kind**
  - Same kind = numbers OR measurements with convertible units (e.g. cm & m; NOT cm & g)
- Notation a : b has **no units**; write map scale as 1 : 10 000, not 1 cm : 100 m
- Distinguish **concept of ratio** from **ratio notation** a : b
- New definition: a : b where **a, b > 0**
- If a quantity = 0, it is meaningless to use ratio

## Rate
- Measures how one quantity **varies with** another (same or different kinds)
- Rate = y/x; if constant → expressed as "y per n units of x"
- **Rate** y/x ≠ **Rate of change** dy/dx in general (equal only when line passes through origin)
- Types: constant rate, step rate, progressive rates, irregular rates
- Rate always compares **two** quantities only

## Ratio vs. Rate
| | Ratio | Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Same kind, 2 quantities | ✓ | ✓ (interconvertible) |
| Same kind, ≥3 quantities | ✓ | ✗ |
| Different kinds, 2 quantities | ✗ | ✓ |

- Ratio a : b (2 quantities) → rate a/b (fraction); vice versa
- Rate comparing same-kind quantities **can** be converted to ratio
- Rate comparing different-kind quantities **cannot** become ratio
- Writing as rate standardises base (= 1), making comparison easier across groups

---

## Proportion
- **Part-whole ratio**: proportion of boys = 3/5 or 60%
- **Part-part ratio**: ratio of boys to girls = 3 : 2
- **Proportionality** (mathematical): a relationship between two quantities — distinct from the above

## Direct Proportion
**Equivalent statements** (all mean y ∝ x):
1. When x × n → y × n (multiplicative reasoning)
2. Equality of two ratios: y₂/y₁ = x₂/x₁
3. Equality of two rates: y₂/x₂ = y₁/x₁
4. Rate y/x = **constant** k (constant of proportionality)
5. y = kx (line through origin); rate of change dy/dx AND rate y/x both = k

> Additive reasoning (constant difference) is NOT sufficient for direct proportion

- Direct proportion is a **special case** of linear relation y = kx + c where **c = 0**

## Inverse Proportion
- y = k/x; equivalently xy = k (constant **product**)
- When x × n → y **÷** n
- Equality of two ratios: y₂/y₁ = x₁/x₂
- Equality of two rates: y₂/x₁ = y₁/x₂
- Rate y/x is **NOT** constant; product xy is constant

---

## Proportionality Across the Curriculum
| Topic | Big Idea Statement |
|---|---|
| Fractions/%, Ratio, Rate | Different representations of proportional relationship |
| Similar triangles | Corresponding sides directly proportional |
| Trigonometry | sin x = opp/hyp constant because similar triangles |
| Bar/pie chart | Bar length / sector area directly proportional to frequency |
| Histogram | Column **area** directly proportional to frequency |
| Unitary method | Applies proportional reasoning without equality of ratios |

---

## Unpacking Content — Pedagogical Reasoning (Shulman, 1987)
**6 Aspects:** Comprehension → Transformation → Instruction → Evaluation → Reflection → New Comprehension

### Transformation (unpack content first, then choose approach)
| Sub-aspect | What it involves |
|---|---|
| **Preparation** | Critically interpret materials; detect errors of omission/commission |
| **Representation** | Analogies, examples, demos to make ideas accessible |
| **Selection** | Choose instructional approach (lecture, discovery, etc.) |
| **Adaptation** | Tailor to students' prior knowledge, misconceptions, language |

> Selection & adaptation come **after** unpacking content — not before

### 5 Elements of Math Content (Backhouse et al., 1992)
| Element | How to teach |
|---|---|
| **Concept** | Explain or develop |
| **Convention** | Just tell (no "why") |
| **Result** | Can be proven |
| **Technique** | Demonstrate with worked examples |
| **Process** | Provide thinking opportunities |

**Key proverb:** *Docendo discimus* — "by teaching, we learn"

# QCM Tutorial Notes — Arithmetic & Algebra

---

## Arithmetic 1 — Numbers

### Syllabus Context
- 3 content strands: Numbers & Algebra · Geometry & Measurement · Statistics & Probability
- 1 process strand: Mathematical Processes
- MOE increasing emphasis on processes — learning experiences (LE) now embedded in textbooks
- N1 (Sec 1 Numbers & Operations) tags: P = Processes, M = Metacognition, A = Attitudes

### Classification of Numbers — Key Pedagogical Points
- **Whole numbers vs natural numbers**: one contains 0, the other doesn't — know which; find tricks to help students remember
- **Positive integers vs non-negative integers**: cleaner, more precise terminology across contexts
- **Even numbers, negative numbers**: students may give correct answers for wrong reasons — get them to *articulate* the definition
- **Perfect squares/cubes**: same issue — ask students to explain, not just identify
- **Rational numbers**: know the definition; know how to prove √2 is irrational
- **Decimal types**: terminating vs non-terminating; recurring vs non-recurring
- **Complex numbers**: real numbers ⊂ complex numbers; imaginary ≠ complex

### Four Operations — Procedural Understanding
- Do students understand *why* long multiplication works? (e.g. why add a zero when multiplying tens?)
- The **distributive law** underlies both long multiplication and the partial quotient algorithm
- Opportunity to surface and address procedural gaps carried over from primary school

---

## Arithmetic 2 — Primes, HCF, LCM

### Definition of Prime Numbers
Three candidate definitions:

| Definition | Problem |
|---|---|
| "A whole number divisible by 1 and itself" | Incomplete — 1 satisfies this but is not prime |
| "A whole number **greater than 1** divisible by 1 and itself" | Correct but verbose |
| "A whole number with **exactly two** different factors" | Clean and precise — preferred |

- The word **exactly** is critical — "at least" changes the meaning entirely
- Analogy: old syllabus trapezium had *exactly* one pair of parallel sides; new syllabus uses *at least* — this changes which shapes qualify and affects the formula ½(a+b)h

### Conceptual Tests (use in class)
- **Stmt 1**: "If a positive integer is not prime, then it must be composite." T/F? Why?
  - False — 1 is neither prime nor composite
- **Stmt 2**: "6 is a composite number." T/F? Why?
  - True — has more than two factors

### Sieve of Eratosthenes
- Purpose: sieve out primes from a list
- **Cannot be used to *introduce* primes** — students need to already know 1 is not prime, and need to understand why uncancelled numbers are prime (still requires factor reasoning)
- Practical note: students are messy — always print a template for them

### Is a Given Number Prime? (Trial Division)
- Divide by each prime up to √n — if none divide exactly, the number is prime
- Rationale: factors come in pairs; if n has a factor > √n, its pair must be < √n, so it would already have been found
- Perfect squares: last factor pair is the same factor (√n × √n)
- This reasoning is beyond most students — teach the procedure, not the proof

### Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic
Every positive integer > 1 is either prime or can be expressed as a **unique** product of primes.

### Number of Divisors Formula
If n = p₁^e₁ × p₂^e₂ × … × pₙ^eₙ, then:

**Total number of positive divisors = (1+e₁)(1+e₂)…(1+eₙ)**

Example: 48 = 2⁴ × 3¹ → (1+4)(1+1) = 10 divisors

### HCF & LCM — Choice of Examples
- **Bad opening example for HCF**: HCF(6, 12) — students may think HCF is always the smaller number
- **Good opening example**: HCF(4, 7) — HCF is 1, challenges assumption
- **Bad opening example for LCM**: LCM(4, 7) — students may think LCM is always the product
- Edge-case examples must still appear during class practice — just not as the first example

### Calculator Trick for HCF
To find HCF(12936, 1260):
1. Compute 12936 ÷ 1260 on calculator — simplifies to 154/15
2. Calculator divided both by their HCF automatically
3. Recover HCF: 1260 ÷ 15 = **84**

### Questioning Technique
- **Choral response**: acceptable when NOT testing for understanding (e.g. recapping)
- **Testing for understanding**: call a specific student
- **Sequence**: ask question → pause ~3 seconds → *then* nominate student
  - If you nominate first, other students stop thinking

### Design of Questions
- Reverse the usual question format to test conceptual understanding
  - E.g. give partial outcome, ask for missing initial info (instead of giving info, asking for outcome)

## Arithmetic 3 — Negative Numbers

### Why Not Rules-Only Teaching
- Negative numbers are among the **most difficult** topics (alongside percentages and fractions)
- Memorising sign rules without understanding leads to misconceptions, especially for lower-readiness students
- Rules (e.g. positive × negative = negative) should be the *last* step, not the first

### Introducing Negative Numbers
**Good real-life contexts:**
- Temperature below 0
- Owing money / deficit / decrease
- Below sea level

**Contexts to avoid:**
- Basement floors (B1, B2) — no zero floor
- BC/AD — no year zero

### Number Line
- Numbers to the right are larger — use this to answer "is −3 bigger than −2?"
- Distinguish operator **minus** (subtraction) from state **negative**:
  - 0 − 2 = −2: read as "zero *minus* two equals *negative* two"

### Algebra Discs — The Five Key Concepts
Teach with algebra discs (concrete) before moving to rules. Teach **one concept + practice** before moving to the next. Do NOT use algebra to generalise during disc teaching — too much cognitive load.

| Concept | Example | Key Idea |
|---|---|---|
| Addition of two negatives | (−2) + (−3) = −5 | Combine negative discs |
| Addition of positive and negative | 5 + (−2) = 5 − 2 | Zero pairs cancel |
| Subtraction between two positives | 3 − 5 = −(5−3) = −2 | Need to add zero pairs |
| Subtracting positive from negative | −3 − 4 = −(3+4) = −7 | Add more negatives |
| Subtraction of a negative | 5 − (−2) = 5 + 2 | Removing negatives = adding positives |

### Lesson Structure for Negative Numbers
1. Teach one concept with discs
2. Class practice on that concept
3. Move to next concept
4. Consolidate: recap all 5 on whiteboard
5. Mixed practice (not arranged in order — students must identify which concept applies)
6. Optional: consolidation puzzle (e.g. secret message — each letter maps to an expression)

### Extension to Algebra
Same five concepts apply:
- (−2x) + (−3x) = −5x
- 5x + (−2x) = 5x − 2x
- 3x − 5x = −2x
- 5x − (−2x) = 5x + 2x

**Note**: lower-readiness students find it harder to transfer from integers to fractions — anticipate this.

### Teaching Multiplication of Negative Numbers
Use disc-flipping model (pictorial is sufficient — no need for physical discs):
- Start: flipping 1 disc gives −1
- Case 1: 2 × 3 — 2 groups of 3
- Case 2: 2 × (−3) — 2 groups of −3
- Case 3: (−3) × 2 — *negative* of 3 groups of 2
- Case 4: (−3) × (−2) — *negative* of 3 groups of −2

**Mental heuristic (sign rules) is the last step** — never open with it.

## Arithmetic 4 — Percentages

### Common Misconceptions
- **Wrong working**: 5/20 = 5/20 × 100 = 25 ⇒ 5/20 = 25 = 25% — must mark this wrong; the expression 5/20 ≠ 25
- **Expressing 0.5% as a fraction**: students confuse 0.5% with 0.5; correct: 0.5% = 0.5/100 = 1/200
- **Base of percentage change**: students frequently identify the 100% (base) incorrectly
  - Profit/loss questions should specify whether to express as % of cost price or selling price
- **Average of percentages**: cannot average percentages unless the bases are equal

## Algebra 1 — Introduction

### Uses of a Letter in Algebra
| Use | Example |
|---|---|
| Specific unknown | Price of apple = x (one fixed unknown value) |
| Variable | y = 2x + 1 (x ranges over values) |
| Parameter | m, c in y = mx + c (fixed for a given line, varies across lines) |

### Types of Algebraic Statements
| Type | Example | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Conditional equation | x + 1 = 0 | True for some values of x |
| Contradiction | x + 1 = x | True for no values |
| Identity | 2x + 3x = 5x | True for all values |
| Function / relation | f(x) = x + 1 or y² = x | Relationship between variables |

### Misconception: Equal Sign
- Students often treat = as "do something to get an answer" — not as equivalence
- This is a **conceptual** misconception, not a presentation issue
- Teach using a balance (physical or virtual) to show equality as balance

### Solving Equations
- Meaning: find value(s) of x such that substituting x makes the equality hold
- Must be stated explicitly — students often solve mechanically without understanding what they are doing

## Algebra 2 — Expansion & Factorisation

### Syllabus Scope
| Level | Content |
|---|---|
| Sec 1 | Expand a(b+c), −a(b+c); factorise ab+ac; solve linear equations incl. simple fractional; word problems |
| Sec 2 | Expand (a+b)(c+d), algebraic identities; factorise by grouping, quadratic expressions; algebraic fractions; simultaneous linear equations; linear inequalities; graphs of linear equations; solve quadratic by factorisation |

- Note: ab (degree 2, two variables) appears in Sec 1; x² (degree 2, one variable) appears in Sec 2

### Action Words in Algebra
**Expand · Simplify · Factorise · Solve** — students must distinguish these; they are different tasks

### Teaching Expansion of a(b+c)
Three approaches:

**C-P-A with tiles:**
- Concrete: physical rectangular tiles/cutouts representing a × (b+c)
- Pictorial: draw the tiles
- Abstract: generalise to a(b+c) = ab + ac
- If pictorial is sufficient, skip concrete

**Algebra discs extension:**
- 2 × 3 = 6 → extend to 2 groups of (x+3)
- Examples and practice → generalise

**Multiplication frame:**
- Handles negative numbers and minus signs — unlike area model
- More versatile than rectangular tile approach

### Teaching Factorisation
- Taught as the **reverse of expansion**
- Students must identify and extract the HCF of terms
- Harder case: −2x − 6 — need to change sign after extracting −2
  - Algebra discs help: flip the discs to show sign change
  - Multiplication frame also handles this

### Quadratic Factorisation (Sec 2)
- Lower-readiness students often cannot see how to factorise quadratics
- Algebra discs: start with all-positive cases, then extend to other cases
- In ax² + bx + c, must state **a ≠ 0**

# QCM Tutorial Notes — Graphs, Functions & Coordinate Geometry

---

## Graphs 1 — Functions & Introduction to Coordinate Geometry

### Why Graphs?
- Graphs link **algebra** and **geometry** together
- Graphs are part of **Coordinate Geometry** (a branch of geometry)
- Quote: *"Algebra is but written geometry and geometry is but figured algebra."* — Sophie Germain (1776–1831)
- Only **Cartesian coordinates** are in the secondary school syllabus (invented by René Descartes, 1596–1650)

### Cartesian Coordinates vs Ordered Pairs
- **Ordered pair**: any pair of numbers where order matters — e.g. Cartesian (x, y), polar (r, θ), event outcomes (H, T)
- **Cartesian coordinates**: a specific type of ordered pair
- Correct grammar: *"The coordinates of P are (2, 3)"* — not "is"

---

## Concept of Function

### Syllabus (LE N6b, Sec 1, p.43)
- Function = **"only one output for every input"**
- Represent functions in 4 forms:
  - **Verbal**: say or write in words
  - **Tabular**: table of values
  - **Graphical**: plot the graph
  - **Algebraic**: equation of function

### Teaching with a Function Machine
- Use a function machine to generate input/output values
- Helps students build intuition before formal definition
- Resources: www.shodor.org/interactivate, www.mathplayground.com/functionmachine

---

## Drawing on Graph Paper

### Problem Areas
- Students may not know what scale to use
- Without a given scale, students tend to draw very small graphs
- **Always give students the scale, domain, and range** — teaching them to choose scale is a separate skill; don't conflate the two

### Orientating Students to Graph Paper
Three shades of green lines:
| Lines | Size |
|---|---|
| Darkest/thickest | 2 cm × 2 cm squares |
| Medium | 1 cm × 1 cm squares |
| Lightest | 2 mm × 2 mm squares |

### Drawing Axes
- Draw axes on the **darkest green line** — easier to read off values (next darkest line = y = 1 or y = 2 depending on scale)
- **One arrow per axis**, at the positive end (local convention — some countries use arrows at both ends)
- Label axes x and y

### How to Teach Graph Drawing
Teach and practise **one skill at a time**:
1. Draw axes → practise
2. Label numbers with scale on axes → practise
3. Plot points → practise
4. Join points → practise

---

## Graphs of Linear Functions

### Syllabus Note — y = ax + b vs y = mx + c
- Sec 1 syllabus uses **y = ax + b**, not y = mx + c
- Students learn *a* = gradient in Sec 1
- Students learn *b* = y-intercept only in **Sec 3** (p.53, G6.3)
- Therefore: **do not teach y-intercept meaning of b in Sec 1** — it's not in scope yet
- Most textbooks use y = mx + c and link c to y-intercept; most teachers don't realise this is a Sec 3 concept

### Spiral Curriculum
- Teach concepts appropriate to the level; revisit at the next level with more depth
- Do **not** bring Sec 3 content down to Sec 1/2
- Different from a modular curriculum (teach everything in one module at one level)

### Exploring Graphs Using Desmos (LE N6c)
- Use sliders at www.desmos.com for y = mx + c
- Keep *m* constant, vary *c* — observe vertical shift
- Keep *c* constant, vary *m* — observe change in steepness
- Start with m > 0, then introduce m < 0

---

## Interpretation of Graphs

### Common Misconception — Graph as Visual Representation
- **Misconception**: a graph is a visual/pictorial representation of a situation
- Example: students asked to draw "height of flag vs time during flag-raising" may draw a **vertical line** (it looks like a flagpole)
- **Correct understanding**: a graph is an **abstract representation** of the *relationship between two variables*, not a picture of the situation

### C-P-A and Graphs
- In C-P-A, it is acceptable to treat graphs as "pictorial" and equations as "abstract"
- But be aware: graphs are not truly visual — they are abstract representations of relationships
- Both are abstractions; graphs are just more visually accessible

## Gradient (Sec 1)

### Developing the Concept
- Draw two line segments with the **same horizontal change** but **different vertical changes**
- Guide students to see that steepness differs because of different vertical changes over the same horizontal change
- Lead to: **gradient = rise / run** (ratio of vertical change to horizontal change)

### Worked Example Sequence for Sec 1
Students are always given a **graph with two points** to calculate gradient.

| WE | Setup | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| WE1 | One point at origin, other in 1st quadrant | Simplest case; skip for stronger classes |
| WE2 | Both points in 1st quadrant, positive gradient | e.g. (1,2) and (5,8) |
| WE3 | Both points in 1st quadrant, negative gradient | Introduce negative gradient |
| WE4 | One point has negative coordinates | Extend to other quadrants |

- After each WE, students practise a **similar question first** before progressing
- Apply **Differentiated Instruction** across WEs

### Additional Gradient Skills to Teach
- **No points given on line**: guide students to choose 2 suitable points themselves — this must be explicitly taught and practised
- **Non-homogeneous scale**: students must know that lines with the same gradient can *look* different if axes have different scales — show examples

### Real-Life Gradient vs Mathematical Gradient
- In real life, gradient is usually positive
- Common misconception: going *up* a hill = positive gradient; going *down* = negative gradient
- **Correction**: gradient has the same sign regardless of direction of travel — it depends on the line, not the walker

### Gradient of Special Lines
- **Horizontal line**: gradient = 0
- **Vertical line**: gradient = undefined
- Students commonly confuse the two and call a horizontal line a "straight line" — clarify that all lines are straight; horizontal is a specific type

---

## Gradient Formula (Sec 3)

### Teaching the Formula
- Use the same WE2: (1, 2) and (5, 8)
- Write x₁, y₁ above (1, 2) and x₂, y₂ above (5, 8) on the graph
- Link rise = y₂ − y₁ and run = x₂ − x₁ to what students already know from Sec 1

### Label Left Point as (x₁, y₁)
- Does it matter which point is labelled (x₁, y₁)? — **No**, formula gives same answer either way
- But convention: label the **left point** as (x₁, y₁) to ensure run = x₂ − x₁ > 0

### Common Problems
- Students substitute wrong values — always write formula before substituting; label coordinates explicitly
- Negative coordinates cause arithmetic errors — encourage use of calculator

### Teaching Negative Gradient with Formula
1. Students practise finding positive gradient using formula
2. Ask: does the formula work for negative gradient?
3. If students say yes → skip WE, go straight to practice
4. If unsure → show example, e.g. (1, 5) and (5, 1):
   - Label left point (x₁, y₁) → run = x₂ − x₁ is positive
   - Draw triangle **above** the line segment for negative gradient
   - Show why rise = y₂ − y₁ is negative

## Length of Line Segment (Sec 3, Coordinate Geometry p.53)

### Developing the Formula
- Scaffold using a right-angled triangle formed by the two points
- Apply Pythagoras' theorem: distance = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]
- Students should derive this — do not just give the formula

## Graphs of Quadratic Functions (Upper Sec)

### Syllabus (N6, p.50)
- Sketch graphs of y = ±(x−p)² + q and y = ±(x−a)(x−b)
- Coefficient of x² = ±1 (but can explore other coefficients)

### Plotting vs Sketching
- **Sketching ≠ rough freehand** — still need scale in some cases to ensure correct shape (e.g. quadratic must be symmetrical)
- To sketch, must find **critical points**:
  - y-intercept
  - x-intercepts (roots)
  - Turning point

### Factorised Form y = ±(x−h)(x−k)

#### x-intercepts
- Do **not** teach "x-intercepts are h and k" directly
- Students will make sign errors: e.g. for y = (x+1)(x+5), they may say x-intercepts are 1 and 5
- Better: just **solve (x+1)(x+5) = 0** to find x-intercepts
- Note: x-intercepts are **numerical values**, not coordinates

#### y-intercept
- Substitute x = 0 into equation

#### Turning Point
- Lies on the **line of symmetry**, which is midway between the two x-intercepts
- x-coordinate of turning point = average of the two x-intercepts
- Link to primary school: average = total ÷ 2 (even-out model)
- Example: y = (x−1)(x−5) → x-intercepts 1 and 5 → turning point x = (1+5)/2 = 3
- Harder example: y = (x+2)(x−3) → x-intercepts −2 and 3 → turning point x = (−2+3)/2 = 0.5
- Substitute x-coordinate into equation to find y-coordinate of turning point
- Drawing line of symmetry on sketch is optional

### Completed Square Form y = ±(x−p)² + q (Sec 3)
- Covered in Sec 3 O-level Maths where coefficient of x² = 1 (N7.10, p.54)
- Typical question: "Express x² − 4x + 1 in the form (x−a)² + b. Hence, sketch y = x² − 4x + 1."
- Critical points found from completed square form directly

## Po-Shen Loh's Method for Quadratic Equations

### Background
- Developed by A/P Loh Po-Shen, Carnegie Mellon University; US IMO national coach
- Reference: www.poshenloh.com/quadratic

### Method (for x² + Bx + C = 0)
1. Sum of roots = −B; product of roots = C
2. Average of roots = −B/2
3. Roots are (−B/2 − u) and (−B/2 + u) — symmetric about line of symmetry
4. Product: (−B/2 − u)(−B/2 + u) = C → (−B/2)² − u² = C → solve for u
5. Recover both roots

**Example**: x² − 6x + 5 = 0
- Sum = 6, average = 3; roots are 3−u and 3+u
- (3−u)(3+u) = 5 → 9 − u² = 5 → u² = 4 → u = ±2
- Roots: x = 1 and x = 5

### Pedagogy Note
- Suitable for **high-progress students** as enrichment — not a replacement for standard methods
- Students should be aware that multiple methods exist
- Weaker parts: finding u from the product equation may still trip students up

### Sum and Product of Roots (No Longer in 2020 A-Math Syllabus)
To develop intuitively (without formal formulae):
- Expand (x−2)(x−3) = x² − 5x + 6
- Observe: sum of roots 2+3 = 5 = **negative of coefficient of x**
- Observe: product of roots 2×3 = 6 = **constant term**

## L2 · Teaching Approaches & Learning Theories 1 (Yeo)

### Skemp's Two Types of Understanding (1976)
| Type | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Relational** | Knowing *what* to do AND *why* |
| **Instrumental** | Rules without reasons |

- Instrumental: students can't adapt or reconstruct when they forget
- Relational: enables reconstruction from understanding; more durable

### Constructivism (Piaget)
- Brain links new knowledge to **existing schema**; weak links = easily forgotten
- **Assimilation**: new info fits existing schema (no change to schema)
- **Accommodation**: schema must change to fit new info
- Students **construct** their own knowledge — not empty vessels

### Skemp's Principle (1987)
> Higher-order concepts cannot be taught by definition — only by **suitable examples**

- Always: **examples first → definition after** (never reverse)
- Definition-first confirms fear that maths is incomprehensible

### Metacognition
- Thinking about thinking: **awareness, monitoring, self-regulation**
- Build in reflection at end of every lesson; one durable takeaway per lesson is enough

## L3 · Use of ICT in Mathematics Education (Yeo)

### Why Use ICT?
> "The computer has the ability to make the abstract concrete." — Turkle & Papert (1990)

ICT bridges abstract concepts and concrete experience (Bruner CPA logic). Use ICT **only when it genuinely enhances student learning** — if manipulatives work better, use those.

### 8 Ways to Use ICT
1. Puzzles / games / songs / videos (engagement — must link back to maths)
2. Pictures (e.g. types of pyramids)
3. **Explore concepts** — GeoGebra, GSP, LiveMath with pre-designed templates
4. Visual aids (e.g. GSP unit circle → sine curve)
5. Teaching aids / emulators (e.g. calculator key presses)
6. Virtual manipulatives (e.g. balance scale for equations)
7. E-learning — must be **interactive**, not just text
8. Prepare materials — Equation Editor, FXDraw3

### Key Rules
- **No PPT for worked examples** — students can't follow; can't ask questions. Use whiteboard.
- PPT is fine for pictures (type 2)
- Give students **pre-designed templates** — building them wastes time on technical aspects, not maths
- **Hands-on > demo**; demo only when content too difficult to explore independently

### Planning an ICT Lesson
- Visit computer lab **a few days before**: test all computers
- Visit again **day/lesson before**: confirm still working
- Try template + worksheet yourself first
- **Conduct**: gather at front → demo → hands-on → demo → hands-on
- **Conclude**: gather to consolidate and verify correct discoveries

### LOVE Framework
**L**inking **O**pportunities in a **V**ariety of **E**xperiences to the learning of maths.
Vary resources to engage students, but always **explicitly link the activity back to mathematics**. Engagement is not the end goal; maths is.

## L4 · Teaching Approaches & Learning Theories 2 (Yeo)

### Bruner's C-P-A Approach
**Concrete → Pictorial → Abstract** (hyphens = linked stages, not 3 separate methods)

- Concrete: physical manipulatives (e.g. algebra tiles, balance scales)
- Pictorial: diagrams, bar models, graphs
- Abstract: symbolic notation, algebra

### Extended C-P-A
When no physical manipulative exists, use **concrete examples (numbers)** as the concrete stage.

| Level | "Concrete" means |
|---|---|
| Primary | Real objects |
| Secondary | Numbers |
| University | Algebra |

What is abstract at one level becomes concrete at a higher level. **Always: concrete/examples → abstract. Never start with abstraction.**

### Teaching Skills Structure ("We do one. You do more.")
Rooted in **behaviourism** (Thorndike, Skinner, Gagné) but modified by constructivism — meaningfulness matters.

1. **WE1** — done together; teacher questions students; leave WE1 on board
2. **Practice Q1** — similar to WE1; WE1 still visible (replaces copying)
3. **Practice Q2+** — slight increase in difficulty/structure
4. **WE2** — introduced when most students can't handle next level; then repeat cycle

- **Homework**: similar difficulty to WEs/practice; 1–2 challenge Qs at end only
- **Copying WEs**: wastes time; Practice Q1 is the model solution
- Must be **deliberate structure and progression** across WEs, practice, and HW

### Attitudes & Dispositions
- **Beliefs → Confidence → Perseverance**: students who think maths = <5 min give up early
- Share real struggle examples; encourage perseverance
- Use variety of resources; aim for at least one interesting part per lesson (LOVE)

## L5 · Unpacking Content in Lesson Planning (Yeo)

### Shulman's Pedagogical Reasoning (1987)
**6-stage cycle:** Comprehension → **Transformation** → Instruction → Evaluation → Reflection → New Comprehension

*Docendo discimus* — "by teaching, we learn"

### Transformation — The Core Teaching Act (4 Sub-aspects)
| Sub-aspect | What it involves |
|---|---|
| **Preparation** | Critically interpret materials; detect errors of omission/commission |
| **Representation** | Analogies, examples, demos to make ideas accessible |
| **Selection** | Choose instructional approach (lecture, discovery, ICT, etc.) |
| **Adaptation** | Tailor to students' prior knowledge, misconceptions, language |

**Critical sequence**: Preparation → Representation → Selection → Adaptation. Unpack content *first*, then choose method. Never choose method before understanding content.

### Backhouse's 5 Elements of Mathematical Content (1992)
| Element | What it is | How to teach |
|---|---|---|
| **Concept** | Idea or relationship | Explain or develop (guided discovery) |
| **Convention** | Arbitrary agreement | Just tell — no "why" |
| **Result** | Theorem or property | Can be proven — show or derive |
| **Technique** | Procedure or algorithm | Demonstrate with worked examples |
| **Process** | Way of thinking | Provide thinking opportunities |

- **Convention**: most commonly mishandled — teachers try to explain "why"; there is no why
- **Process**: most commonly neglected — cannot be taught by demo; students must do the thinking

## L6 · Big Ideas in Mathematics (Yeo)

### What are Big Ideas?
Central ideas that **link topics, strands, and levels** into a coherent whole (Charles, 2005). Taught by forming **statements about** a big idea — not by defining the big idea itself.

### 8 Secondary Clusters (IF DN PM ME) - if doing nothing PM me
**F**unctions · **I**nvariance · **N**otations · **D**iagrams · **P**roportionality · **M**easures · **M**odels · **E**quivalence
^ Oops, the above order is wrong for the mnemonic.

Primary: 6 clusters — exclude Functions & Models

**Top 3 (lecturer's view):** Proportionality · Equivalence · Functions

### Ratio
- Compares **relative sizes** of ≥2 quantities of the **same kind**
- Same kind = numbers or convertible units (cm & m ✓; cm & g ✗)
- Notation a : b has **no units**; write map scale as 1 : 10 000 not 1 cm : 100 m
- New definition: a, b > 0 — ratio is meaningless if a quantity = 0

### Rate
- Measures how one quantity **varies with** another (same or different kinds)
- Always compares **exactly two** quantities; rate = y/x
- Rate (y/x) ≠ Rate of change (dy/dx) — equal only when line passes through origin
- Writing as rate standardises base to 1 — easier to compare across groups

### Ratio vs Rate
| | Ratio | Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Same kind, 2 quantities | ✓ | ✓ (interconvertible) |
| Same kind, ≥3 quantities | ✓ | ✗ |
| Different kinds, 2 quantities | ✗ | ✓ |

### Direct Proportion (y ∝ x) — 5 Equivalent Statements
1. x × n → y × n (multiplicative reasoning)
2. y₂/y₁ = x₂/x₁ (equality of two ratios)
3. y₂/x₂ = y₁/x₁ (equality of two rates)
4. y/x = k (constant of proportionality)
5. y = kx (line through origin; dy/dx = y/x = k)

> **Additive reasoning (constant difference) ≠ direct proportion**

Direct proportion is a special case of y = kx + c where **c = 0**

### Inverse Proportion
- y = k/x; equivalently **xy = k** (constant product)
- x × n → y ÷ n
- Rate y/x is NOT constant; **product** xy is constant

### Proportionality Across the Curriculum
| Topic | Connection |
|---|---|
| Fractions, %, ratio, rate | Different representations of same proportional relationship |
| Similar triangles | Corresponding sides directly proportional |
| Trigonometry | sin x = opp/hyp constant because of similar triangles |
| Bar/pie charts | Bar length / sector area proportional to frequency |
| Histogram | Column **area** (not height) proportional to frequency |
| Unitary method | Proportional reasoning without formally writing equality of ratios |

## Problem Solving (L2 + Pentagon)

### What is a Mathematical Problem?
- No immediate procedure available
- Solver must **accept** it (engage willingly) and **attempt** it (requires motivation)
- Distinguishes a *problem* from an *exercise*

### Polya's 4-Step Strategy
1. **Understand** the problem — what is given, unknown, conditions?
2. **Devise a plan** — select a heuristic
3. **Carry out the plan** — execute; loop back if it fails
4. **Check and expand** — verify answer; reflect; consider extensions

Process is **non-linear** — looping is expected.

### Heuristics (taught explicitly)
Draw a diagram · Work backwards · Look for a pattern · Solve a simpler problem · Make a list/table · Guess and check

> Basic knowledge must be **memorised** to free cognitive capacity for heuristic thinking (Schoenfeld)

### 4 Modes of Learning Maths
| Mode | Vehicle |
|---|---|
| Through **practice** | Exercises — consolidate known procedures |
| **Through** problem solving | Hard problems — struggle builds new understanding |
| **For** problem solving | Applications — content learned then applied |
| **About** problem solving | Explicit teaching of heuristics and strategies |
